Mole Negro

Oaxaca, Mexico — a sauce that carries civilizations

Serves 6–8  ·  Prep 2 hrs  ·  Cook 3 hrs

Flavor Profile

Taste: Deep, bitter-sweet, smoky, with a slow-building warmth. Not spicy-hot, but warm in a way that sits behind the throat.

Smell: Toasted chili, burnt tortilla, dark chocolate, a whisper of cinnamon and clove.

Spice Notes: Charred pasilla negro, chilhuacle negro, Mexican cinnamon (canela), black pepper, clove, cumin, dried thyme and oregano, and unsweetened chocolate.

Origin & History

A Sauce Written in History

🏔️ Oaxaca · Sierra Madre

Oaxaca, Mexico — homeland of mole negro

The word mole comes from the Nahuatl mōlli, simply meaning sauce or stew. It predates the Aztec empire, threaded back through the Zapotec and Mixtec kitchens of the Oaxacan valleys — a region so geographically complex, so mountainous and isolated, that it preserved its own languages, its own corn varieties, its own chili cultivars, and consequently its own cuisine with a ferocity no colonizer could fully erase.

Oaxaca grows its own distinct chilies: the smoky chilhuacle negro, the fruity pasilla oaxaqueño, the raisin-dark mulato. These are not interchangeable with chilies from other Mexican regions, and any mole negro that calls itself Oaxacan is built on them. This specificity of ingredient is not culinary pride — it is geography made edible.

The Spanish arrival in the 16th century did something unexpected to mole: it deepened it. The colonizers brought cinnamon, black pepper, clove, almonds, and lard. The Oaxacans absorbed these and folded them into existing preparations without abandoning their own. The result was not fusion in the modern sense — it was negotiation. Every ingredient in mole negro marks a point in that negotiation.

The ingredient most debated is chocolate. Many sources, particularly those written for foreign audiences, romanticize chocolate as the secret of mole. The truth is more layered. Pre-Columbian cacao was bitter, ceremonial, and not universally used in savory preparations. The chocolate in modern mole negro — specifically the chocolate negro or Mexican drinking chocolate — is there in small quantities, and its function is tannin and bitterness, not sweetness.

There is also a burned element unique to mole negro. The chili seeds, and sometimes a charred tortilla or piece of chile itself, are deliberately blackened until they smoke. This is not an accident or a shortcut — it is a technique that predates written recipe culture. The ash adds a flavor that has no direct European analog: dark, slightly vegetal, definitively Oaxacan.

Mole negro is a celebration food. It appears at weddings, Day of the Dead, major holidays. In Oaxaca it is served over turkey, as it has been for centuries, though chicken adapts well for home kitchens. It is never a quick preparation — three hours is optimistic, and some family versions stretch over two days. The labor is the ritual.

Versions & Interpretations

Mole negro differs village to village, family to family. In some versions, raisins add a fruity undercurrent; in others, plantain is roasted and folded in. City restaurants in Oaxaca City often streamline the preparation; village grandmothers use a stone metate and grind everything by hand over hours. There are seven canonical moles of Oaxaca — negro, rojo, coloradito, amarillo, verde, chichilo, and manchamanteles — and negro is considered the most complex. Outside Mexico, it appears in Chicano family tables, in high-end restaurants across the US, and in jarred pastes on grocery shelves. The paste is never the same thing, but it is a starting point.

Ingredients

What You Need

These measurements are guidelines. This is grandma cooking — eyeball, adjust, taste as you go.

The Chilies

  • 5–6 dried chilhuacle negro chilies, stems removed
  • 3–4 dried pasilla negro chilies, stems removed
  • 2 dried mulato chilies (or ancho as substitute)
  • Seeds from 2–3 of the chilies (kept for charring)

The Base

  • 4 medium tomatoes, roughly chopped
  • 4 tomatillos, husked
  • 1 medium white onion, quartered
  • 6 garlic cloves, unpeeled
  • 1 corn tortilla (stale is better), torn up
  • Handful of raw peanuts or almonds
  • 2 tbsp raisins
  • 2 tbsp sesame seeds

Spices & Seasonings

  • 1 small stick Mexican cinnamon (canela)
  • 4 whole cloves
  • 1 tsp black peppercorns
  • 1 tsp cumin seeds
  • 1 tsp dried oregano (Mexican preferred)
  • 2–3 sprigs fresh thyme
  • 30g dark chocolate (70%+) or Mexican chocolate tablet
  • Salt to taste
  • 2–3 cups good chicken or turkey stock
  • 2 tbsp lard or neutral oil for frying

To Serve

  • 1 whole chicken (or 1.2kg bone-in pieces), poached
  • Sesame seeds, for garnish
  • White rice or fresh tortillas
Method

Steps

  1. Toast the dried chilies in a dry skillet over medium heat, pressing them flat, for 20–30 seconds per side until fragrant and slightly darkened. Do not burn. Transfer to a bowl, cover with boiling water and soak 30 minutes until softened. Reserve soaking water.
  2. In the same dry skillet over high heat, char the chili seeds until blackened and smoking. This takes 2–3 minutes. Set aside — this is the secret darkness of the sauce.
  3. Char the tomatoes, tomatillos, onion, and garlic (still in skin) directly in the dry skillet until deeply blackened in spots. The garlic should take 8–10 minutes total. Peel the garlic once cool enough to handle.
  4. Toast the spices (cinnamon, cloves, peppercorns, cumin) in the dry skillet until fragrant, about 1–2 minutes. Then toast the sesame seeds and nuts separately until golden. Finally, fry the tortilla pieces in a little oil or lard until dark brown and crispy.
  5. Blend the soaked chilies with some soaking water until smooth. Strain if you prefer silky; leave it for more body. Blend the charred vegetables, burnt seeds, toasted spices, nuts, tortilla, and raisins together with stock until as smooth as your blender allows.
  6. Heat lard in a deep heavy pot over high heat. Add the chili purée and fry it, stirring constantly, for 5 minutes — it will spit and darken. Then add the vegetable and spice blend. Fry and stir another 5–8 minutes until the sauce thickens and the fat begins to separate at the edges.
  7. Add the remaining stock, the chocolate, oregano, thyme, and salt. Reduce heat to low. Simmer uncovered, stirring occasionally, for 45 minutes to 1 hour. The sauce should coat a spoon thickly. Taste and adjust salt.
  8. Add the poached chicken pieces to the sauce. Simmer together another 20 minutes so the meat absorbs the mole. Serve over rice or with tortillas, garnished with sesame seeds.
Storage

How Long It Keeps

Refrigerator

The mole sauce keeps for up to 5 days in the fridge in a sealed container. The flavor continues to develop — day two is often better than day one.

Freezer: Mole negro freezes exceptionally well. Portion into bags or containers and freeze for up to 3 months. Defrost overnight in the fridge and reheat gently with a splash of stock.

Note: Store the sauce separately from the chicken if possible — the chicken can be reheated directly in the sauce when serving.
