Freshly wrapped Zongzi · Makes 6

Dragon Boat Festival 2025 China Ancient China

Sticky Rice Dumplings

端午节's Zongzi — rice sealed in leaves, bound by string, carried by legend

Serves 6 (3 sweet, 3 savory)  ·  Soak overnight  ·  Cook 10–20 min

Flavor Profile

Sweet: Earthy, nutty red beans plus caramel-like chewy dates with plain rice.

Savory: Deep umami from five-spice braised pork, egg, and mushrooms, plus salted egg yolk with dark, soy-seasoned rice for a rich, slightly briny bite.

Aroma: Faintly sweet bamboo leaf, toasted five-spice, and a warm steamed-rice scent that fills the whole kitchen.

Origin & History

A Legend Sealed in Leaves

A quiet moment — tea and Zongzi, tied with red string

Zongzi dates back over 2,000 years to the Spring and Autumn Period (771–476 BCE) in Ancient China — an incredibly interesting and theatrical era in its own right. The tradition as we know it today, however, is tied to the Warring States Period that followed, and to one man in particular: Qu Yuan, a devoted poet and minister who drowned himself in the Miluo River in 278 BCE after his kingdom fell.

In the text Xu Qi Xie Ji, the ghost of Qu Yuan is said to have appeared in a dream to a man named Ou Hui, instructing him to seal rice inside leaves and bind them with colored string — a way to repel the dragons that would otherwise consume offerings meant for Qu Yuan's spirit. Today, people simply eat the food themselves and celebrate. A development I, for one, am not mad about at all.

Zongzi come in many flavors and variations, but typically consist of glutinous rice wrapped in bamboo leaves into a triangular shape, then cooked through. Sweet and savory are the two most common styles. Sweet zongzi (tian zongzi) is more common in southern China and Taiwan, while savory versions — packed with braised pork and salted egg yolk — are beloved throughout Southeast Asia, in cuisines like Malaysian and Singaporean cooking. There is also a Northern Chinese version stuffed with red dates.

Star ingredient — bamboo leaves: bamboo itself has always been a significant part of Chinese culture, but wrapping zongzi in bamboo leaf isn't just for show. The leaf carries its own faint, sweet aroma that pairs wonderfully with glutinous rice, lending a warm, subtle scent that becomes the perfect canvas for any filling. Bamboo leaves are also naturally antibacterial — an ancient method of food preservation in itself.

By the Jin Dynasty (266–420 CE), zongzi was formally recognized in literature for both ritual and dietary uses, and the name had shifted from jiaoshu to zongzi. Remarkably, in 1983, archaeologists found actual zongzi inside a Han dynasty tomb — over 2,100 years old. Someone packed zongzi for the afterlife. Honestly, a wise move.

Five-spice aromatics for the savory braise

Versions & Interpretations

I couldn't decide on the flavors, so I made all of them. This recipe contains two versions — Sweet Red Bean and Date, and Savory Braised Meat Zongzi. Personally, while zongzi is delicious, I think it's a little heavy to be an everyday staple — and that's exactly what makes it the perfect celebratory food for special occasions, rather than a daily one.

Ingredients

What You Need

Makes about 6 Zongzi (3 sweet, 3 savory). Measurements are guidelines — this is grandma cooking, eyeball and adjust as you go.

The Base

  • 2 cups glutinous rice
  • 12 dried bamboo leaves
  • 1/2 roll kitchen twine or reed strips

Sweet Flavor

  • 1/2 cup dried red beans
  • 6 dried red dates (jujubes), pitted
  • 1 1/2 tablespoons sugar
  • 1/2 teaspoon oil
  • Zest of 1/2 an orange

Savory Flavor

  • 3 salted duck egg yolks
  • 150g pork belly, cut into small chunks
  • 3 braised quail eggs (optional)
  • 3 dried shiitake mushrooms, soaked (optional)
  • 3 chestnuts (optional)

Braising Brine & Seasoning

  • 1 tablespoon soy sauce
  • 1/2 tablespoon dark soy sauce
  • 1/2 tablespoon oyster sauce
  • 1 tablespoon rice wine
  • 1/5 teaspoon five-spice powder (or seasoning sachet)
  • 1/2 teaspoon sugar (for pork marinade)
  • 1 tablespoon sesame oil (for savory rice)
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt (for savory rice)
  • 2 tablespoons braising brine (from the pork pot)
Method

Steps

The Night Before

  1. Soak the dried bamboo leaves and dried red beans in separate bowls of cold water. Cover and refrigerate overnight.
  2. Soak the glutinous rice in cold water for at least 5 hours (or overnight alongside the beans). Drain before use.

Ingredient Prep

  1. Braise the pork: the next day, put the pork in a pot with soy sauce, dark soy sauce, oyster sauce, rice wine, five-spice powder (or sachet), and sugar. Mix well, cover, and simmer for at least 2 hours. Reserve the braising brine. Optionally add shiitake mushrooms and peeled boiled quail eggs to the pot while braising.
  2. Cook the red bean filling: drain the soaked red beans and simmer in fresh water until soft but not mushy, about 30 minutes. Drain, then stir in sugar, oil, and orange zest. Set aside to cool.

Assembly

  1. Season the rice: drain the soaked glutinous rice and divide into two equal portions. Season one half with salt and sesame oil for the savory base, then add braising brine from the pork pot for extra depth. Leave the other half plain for the sweet zongzi.
  2. Prepare the bamboo leaves: drain the soaked leaves and wipe dry. Identify the smoother side — this faces inward when wrapping.
  3. Make a leaf cone: overlap two leaves to form a wide surface, then curl them at the middle into a cone shape — think paper water cups. Pinch the base so no rice can escape.
  4. Fill the cone: pack in one spoon of rice, then your fillings (dates and red bean for sweet; yolk, pork, and optionally chestnut, mushroom, and egg for savory, with their respective rice). Add more rice on top, like a rice sandwich, until about 80% full.
  5. Fold and tie — the origami finale: fold the top of the leaves down over the rice, pinch both sides, and tuck the leaf tip inside the fold of the cone. It should look something like a pyramid... should. Wrap and tie firmly with kitchen twine or reed strips. Give it a gentle shake — if it rattles, unwrap and try again. A good zongzi has no secrets and no gaps. This takes practice; your first one might fall apart, but keep calm and wrap on, it looks harder than it actually is.
  6. Boil or steam the zongzi: submerge in a pot or place in a steamer and cover. Bring to a boil over high heat, then reduce to a steady simmer. Cover and cook for 10–20 minutes until soft.
  7. Unwrap and enjoy: carefully remove from the pot and let cool for 5 minutes (or not — the steam inside is very hot and eager to escape). Snip the twine, unfold the leaves, and reveal your pyramid of sticky, fragrant rice. The savory zongzi needs no accompaniment; some like to eat the sweet ones with a side dish of granulated sugar.
Notes & Tips

No bamboo leaves? Skip the wrapping entirely — layer all the ingredients in a rice cooker and cook as normal. You'll get the same flavors with none of the folding. Alternatively, lay the leaves loosely on top of the rice and fillings before cooking in the rice cooker.

Wrapping tip: practice on the sweet zongzi first, since the filling is less messy than the savory version. The savory zongzi requires a little more prep and the ingredients can be harder to source, but the result is well worth it.

Storage

How Long It Keeps

Refrigerator & Freezer

Cooked zongzi keeps in the fridge for up to 5 days; uncooked zongzi keeps even longer. Stick them in the freezer for up to 3 months — many stores sell them ready-made that way, which makes zongzi the original meal-prep champion.

To reheat: steam or simmer in shallow water for 10 minutes, or microwave (still in leaves, loosely wrapped) for 3–4 minutes — not too long, or they might get mushy.
